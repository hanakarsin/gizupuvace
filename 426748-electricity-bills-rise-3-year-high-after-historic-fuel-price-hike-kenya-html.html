Electricity Bills Rise to 3-Year High After Historic Fuel Price Hike in Kenya

Publish date: 2024-05-30
  • Fuel cost charge has risen from KSh 3.77 in August, last month, to KSh 3.88 this month, September 2021
  • For KSh 1,000, Kenyans will now get 39.82 units, down from 40.13 they were getting previously
  • This comes amid protests from Kenyans who have been also decrying the rising cost of living

Kenyans will get fewer electricity units compared to last month, following the revision of prices by the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA).

According to EPRA, electricity bills have reached a 38-month high, on account of increase in fuel surcharge levied on electricity tariffs.

The Authority said the fuel cost charge has risen from KSh 3.77 in August, last month, to KSh 3.88 this month, the highest since July 2018.

As such, Kenyans who pay KSh 1,000 for electricity tokens will now receive 39.82 units, down from 40.13 they were getting previously, according to a report by Business Daily.

The increase in cost of electricity will most likely unleash pricing pressure across the economy as producers of services and goods factor in the higher cost of power.

Fuel prices hit record high in latest review

The hike in electricity prices come barely a week after the Authority increased the pump prices to the highest levels ever witnessed in the country.

Super Petrol prices increased by 6% to KSh 134.7 per litre, from KSh 127.1 per litre, while diesel prices increased by 7.4% to KSh 115.6 per litre, from KSh 107.7 per litre.

Kerosene prices increased by 13.2% to KSh 110.8 per litre, from KSh 97.9 per litre.

Analysts from Cytonn Investments attributed the rise to several issues key among the removal of fuel subsidies of KSh 7.1 on Super Petrol, KSh 9.9 on Diesel and KSh 11.4 on Kerosene.

The fuel price hike was also attributed to increase in the Free on Board (FOB) price of Murban crude oil in August 2021 by 8.5% to USD 72.3 per barrel, from USD 66.7 per barrel in July 2021, among other factors.

New prices met with public rage

As earlier reported by TUKO.co.ke, Kenyans angrily protested the increase in fuel prices saying it was unfair for the government to push the prices that high, considering they were still facing the negative impact of the COVID-19 pandemic.

Njeri Ndung’u for instance, said she was still operating with a reduced salary following a pay cut that was issued by her employer in the wake of the pandemic.

“I know the new fuel price will reflect on matatu fares, which is really unfair,” she said.
