KEMSA Scandal: Joshua Kutuny Challenges David Murathe to Face MPs, Clear His Name

- The company was awarded KSh 4 billion for the supply of COVID-19 450,000 personal protective equipment at KSh 9,000 each

- Cheranganyi said it would be prudent if Murathe appeared before a parliamentary committee to clear his name

Jubilee Party vice-chairperson David Murathe could face the parliament after his name allegedly featured in Kenya Medical Supply (KEMSA) scandal investigations.

Vocal Cheranganyi MP Joshua Kutuny who is a fierce member of the ruling outfit said it would be prudent if Murathe appeared before a parliamentary committee to clear his name.

"It has come to public attention that vice-chairman of our political party has been adversely been mentioned in the ongoing investigation by Public Investment Committee, I urge the vice-chairman Hon David Murathe to appear before the committee and clear his name," he said on Friday, April 23.

The National Assembly Public Investments Committee probing KSh7.8 billion spent by the drug agency in irregular COVID-19 tender heard that Murathe could have influenced the award of the tender to one of the companies.

In a written submission to the committee, Murathe admitted he was a signatory of Kilig Company Limited accounts and no the director as had been previously suspected.

The company was awarded KSh 4 billion for the supply of COVID-19 450,000 personal protective equipment units at a cost of KSh 9,000 each.

Can't remember, won't remember

On March 10, the company director Ivy Minyow Onyango told the committee said she could not remember bank account numbers or signatories.

The committee was taken aback by Onyango's statements since she was listed as the sole director of the company.

“Kindly allow me to provide clear answers to these questions to avoid a situation where I may say something that is not accurate. I am ready but I still don’t have responses the committee is seeking,” she told the MPs.

At one point, Onyango got into a heated argument with committee members, prompting Abdulswamad Nassir's team to send her to a five-minute break to consult.

Despite making several phone calls, she insisted that she did not have an accurate response about the firm's signatories and bank accounts.
